The War That Never Ends: Las Ladrones Highlands

In the remote interior, the government says it is fighting terrorists. The locals say they are fighting for their existence. A special report from behind the lines.
By James Morrell  |  September 20, 2025

The checkpoint at KM 44 marks the end of the state's effective authority. Beyond that rusted barrier, the rules change. The highlands of Las Ladrones have always been a world apart, but the conflict currently burning through the cordillera is different from the skirmishes of the past.

It is driven by identity, land, and a deep, historic resentment of the lowland capital. The Ladrones Peoples Party (LPP) has morphed from a Marxist relic into a broad-based ethno-nationalist front. They are no longer just ambushing patrols; they are governing.

"They are no longer just ambushing patrols; they are governing."

In the villages I visited, the educational curriculum is LPP-approved. The courts are village tribunals sanctioned by the Party. And the tax collectors wear fatigues.

The government's response has been a "scorched earth" campaign of aerial bombardment and hamletting. It is a strategy that military observers say is creating more insurgents than it kills. For every village flattened, a new recruitment center opens.

As the conflict drags into its fifth decade, the question is not who will win, but what will be left of the nation when the shooting finally stops.
